Doctrine of the Sustaining Ministry of God the Holy Spirit to the Incarnate Christ

  1. Definition: During the Incarnation, the humanity of Jesus Christ was sustained by the indwelling presence and filling of God the Holy Spirit from His birth to His death and resurrection.
  2. The ministry of God the Holy Spirit to the Incarnate Christ was prophesied in the Old Testament (Isa. 11:2; 42:1; 61:1).
  3. The sustaining ministry of God the Holy Spirit and the virgin birth:
    1. The Holy Spirit was the agent of conception in the virgin birth of Christ (Matt. 1:18, 20).
    2. The virgin conception of Christ fulfilled the prophecy in Psalm 40:6 and quoted in Hebrews 10:5b, "A BODY THOU HAST PREPARED FOR ME."
  4. The total ministry of God the Holy Spirit to the humanity of Christ:
    1. The Holy Spirit is given "without measure" to the humanity of Christ (John 3:34).
    2. The Holy Spirit not only indwelt the humanity of Christ as royalty, but He also filled or controlled the soul of the humanity of Christ.
  5. The Holy Spirit related to the baptism of Christ (Matt. 3:13–17):
    1. The water in Christ's baptism represented the will of God the Father for the Son in the First Advent.
    2. Christ's baptism was His identification with and dedication to do the will of the Father in going to the cross.
    3. The dove represented the omnipotence of God the Holy Spirit who would sustain Him.
  6. The Holy Spirit related to the public ministry of Christ (Isa. 42:1; Matt. 12:18):
    1. Jesus performed miracles through the sustaining ministry of God the Holy Spirit, not through the independent function of His own deity (Matt. 12:28).
    2. The teaching ministry of Christ was performed in the power of the Holy Spirit (Luke 4:14–15).
  7. The Holy Spirit sustained Jesus Christ in the prototype spiritual life while He bore our sins in His own body on the cross (Heb. 9:14).1
  8. The Holy Spirit participated in the resurrection of Jesus Christ:
    1. God the Holy Spirit returned Christ's soul from Hades to His body in the grave (Rom. 8:11a).
    2. The Holy Spirit sustained Christ during His post-resurrection trip to Tartarus to announce His strategic victory to the incarcerated fallen angels (1 Pet. 3:18–19).
  9. The sustaining ministry of the Holy Spirit is transferred from the resurrected glorified Christ at the right hand of the Father to the royal family of God who are commanded to avail themselves of this ministry (Eph. 5:18; Gal. 5:16). Jesus Christ was sustained as perfect royalty; believer is sustained as sinful royalty (Eph. 3:16–19).
  10. The purpose of the sustaining ministry of God the Holy Spirit in the Church Age is the glorification of Christ (John 7:39; 16:14; 1 Cor. 6:19–20).

Doctrine of the Ministry of the Holy Spirit

  1. Ministry of the Holy Spirit in the Old Testament.
    1. Regeneration (John 3:5–8; Titus 3:5; 1 Pet. 1:23).
    2. Enduement—enabled a few divinely appointed believers to perform special functions (Gen. 41:38; Ex. 28:3; Judg. 3:10; 1 Sam. 10:10) and could be removed for discipline (Ps. 51:11).
  2. Ministry of the Holy Spirit in the Church Age.
    1. Salvation ministry.
      1. Efficacious grace (Eph. 2:8–9)
      2. Regeneration (John 3:1–16; Titus 3:5).
      3. Baptism (Acts 1:5; 1 Cor. 12:13; Eph. 4:5).
      4. Indwelling (Rom. 8:9; 1 Cor. 6:19–20).
      5. Sealing (2 Cor. 1:22; Eph. 1:13; 4:30).
      6. Distribution of spiritual gifts (1 Cor. 12:11).
    2. Postsalvation ministry: the filling of the Holy Spirit that produces spirituality (Eph. 5:18).
      1. Spirituality and carnality, mutually exclusive absolutes (1 John 3:4–9).
      2. Spirituality lost through carnality; recovered through rebound (Prov. 1:23; 1 John 1:9).
      3. Spirituality, a function of the royal priesthood—not subject to Levitical priesthood of Mosaic Law (Rom. 8:2–4; 10:4; 13:8; Gal. 5:18).
      4. Character of incarnate Christ produced in believer (Gal. 4:19; 5:22–23).
      5. Not characterized by emotion or tabooism (Rom. 16:17–18; 2 Cor. 6:11–12).
      6. General objectives of spirituality.
        1. Partakers of the divine nature (2 Pet. 1:4).
        2. Imitation of God (2 Cor. 3:3; Eph. 5:1).
        3. Glorification of Christ (John 7:39; 16:14; 1 Cor. 6:19–20).
        4. Fulfillment of the Law (Rom. 8:2–4).
        5. Proper function of faith perception—assimilation of Bible doctrine (John 14:26; 16:12–14; 1 Cor. 2:9–16).
  3. Ministry of the Holy Spirit to the unbeliever.
    1. Restraining (2 Thess. 2:7).
    2. Convicting (John 16:7–11).
    3. Regenerating (John 3:5).

  2. The Concept of Divine Personality

    1. Essence is not only what God is by way of His character, but what He is by way of His personality.
    2. Mankind has personality because he is made in the image of God, that is, having real but invisible attributes (which, of course, are not the same as God's attributes).
    3. The statement that "God created man in His own image" (Gen. 1:27) does not imply that man was created as a duplication of God, to share either His deity or physical appearance. "God created man in His own image" connotes that man possesses all the attributes of personality, not that man possesses deity.
    4. Man's personality is based on the essence of his soul. The soul with its characteristics is internal; the personality is external. Personality is a manifestation of how the soul functions.
    5. There are many people in the human race, but all have the same essence. We might say that mankind is one in essence but multifarious in personality. The Trinity is one in essence but only three in personality. Each member of the Godhead is a personality.
    6. While mankind is possessed of the essential elements of personality and is capable of their normal exercise within a limited sphere, God is the source and embodiment of these essential elements which He possesses in the sphere of infinity and to an unlimited degree.
    7. Nearly every page of the Bible asserts the personality of God. We derive our understanding of the personality of God from the manifestation of various combinations of His attributes throughout the Word of God.
    8. God designs, executes, and empowers; these are activities of His personality.
    9. God thinks and makes decisions. These are activities of the person of God; the superstructure of His essence.
    10. The most basic parts of personality are self-consciousness and self-determination. One must first be aware that he exists, then he can make decisions in relationship to that awareness.
    11. God speaks of Himself as "I" or "I, even I" many times in Scripture (Gen. 6:17; Ex. 20:2; Lev. 26:28; Isa. 43:11, 25). Therefore, God is self-conscious.
    12. God expresses His thoughts, desires, and decisions, therefore He has self-determination (Ex. 20:2–17; Isa. 55:8–9).
    13. God requires integrity and perfect rationality. Personality acts with perfect integrity and rationality. Rationality means you think, and integrity means you apply what you think.

    1. The term hypostatic is derived from the compound Greek word ὑπόστασις (hypostasis) meaning “actual being, essence, substantial nature, setting or placing under, that which has actual existence, taking a thing on oneself.”
    2. The compound Greek word is derived from ὑπο (hypo) meaning “under” and στασις (stasis) meaning “to stand.”
      1. Hypostasis therefore comes to mean “to stand under, to take a characteristic upon oneself.”
      2. Jesus Christ took upon Himself as deity true humanity (John 1:1–3; Rom. 1:3–5; 1 Tim. 3:16).
    3. The union consummated at the virgin birth or First Advent is a personal or hypostatic union in that Christ is one person, not two.
      1. Jesus Christ is at the same time God and man with no mixture of the two natures to form a third substance or hypostasis.
      2. The divine nature always remains the divine nature and the human nature always remains the human nature.
  3. The Lord Jesus Christ is unique in the universe.
    1. He is the God-man.
      1. He is undiminished deity and true humanity in one person forever.
      2. He is different from the other members of the Trinity in that He is true humanity.
      3. He is different from mankind in that He is eternal God.
    2. Since the First Advent, it is no longer the divine nature alone which is expressed in His person, but the human nature as well.
  4. The hypostatic union refers to the whole person of Jesus Christ as distinguished from His two natures.
    1. The word nature is derived from the Greek participle οὐσία (ousia) of the verb εἰμί (eimi) meaning “to be.” Ousia means “being, existing,” but comes to mean “nature.”
    2. The two natures of Christ maintain their complete identity though being joined in personal union forever.
      1. It is impossible to transfer an attribute of one nature to the other nature without destroying that nature.
      2. To take from Christ a single attribute of His deity would destroy His deity.
      3. To take from Christ a single attribute of His humanity would destroy His humanity.
      4. Each nature has its own attributes which are inherent to that nature.
      5. All the attributes of deity adhere to His deity and never cross over and become humanity.
      6. All the attributes of humanity adhere to His humanity and never cross over and become deity.
    3. In the hypostatic union of Christ, no attribute of divine essence is compromised or changed, diminished or destroyed. The essence of God is immutable (Heb. 13:8).
    4. However in fulfilling God the Father’s plan, purpose, and policy for the Incarnation certain attributes of Christ were not used (Phil. 2:5–8).
      1. “Emptied” is derived from the Greek κενόω (kenoo) and means “to deprive oneself of a rightful function,” and refers to Jesus Christ’s willing restriction of the independent use of His divine attributes (Heb. 2:6–10; 10:5–7).
      2. While Christ retained all the attributes of His divine nature, He never used His divine attributes to benefit, provide, or to glorify Himself (Matt. 4:3–4).
  5. Jesus Christ is God with all the attributes of God and He is perfect humanity. The two natures remain distinct, whole, unchanged, without mixture or confusion, so that Jesus Christ remains forever truly God and truly man.
    1. Jesus Christ is eternal God.
      1. All divine titles are ascribed to Christ: “God” (John 1:1); “God, the Almighty” (Rev. 19:6); God “over all” (Rom. 9:5); “our great God” (Titus 2:13); and “Lord,” the Greek word κύριος (kurios) for deity (Phil. 2:11).
      2. All divine attributes are ascribed to Christ.
        1. He declared His eternal existence (John 8:58).
        2. He is declared to be the creator and sustainer of the universe (Col. 1:16–17); He “upholds all things by the word of His power” (Heb. 1:3).
        3. He is immutable (Heb. 13:8); and the “radiance of His [God’s] glory” (Heb. 1:3).
        4. Jesus Christ declared that He and the Father are one in essence (John 8:58; 10:30).
      3. As eternal, infinite God, Jesus Christ is coequal with God the Father and God the Holy Spirit (John 10:30; 2 Cor. 13:14).
      4. As eternal, infinite God, Jesus Christ is superior to both angels and mankind (Deut. 6:4; Heb. 1:2).
    2. Jesus Christ is true humanity.
      1. The humanity of Christ is said to have flesh and blood (Heb. 2:14; 1 John 4:2).
      2. Jesus Christ was born into the human race perfect—without a sin nature, and trichotomous—body, soul, and spirit—through the virgin pregnancy and birth (Matt. 1:20).
      3. Jesus was fully aware of His deity from birth (Heb. 10:5–10).
      4. Jesus had normal physical development (Luke 2:52).
      5. Jesus experienced pleasure and rest (Matt. 8:25; 9:10); suffered pain, sorrow, hunger, thirst, fatigue, and death (Matt. 4:2; 14:14; John 11:35; 19:28, 33).
      6. After three days in the grave, Jesus was raised from the dead and received a resurrection body (Matt. 16:21; 28:6).
      7. The humanity of Jesus Christ was temptable, but remained sinless (2 Cor. 5:21; Heb. 4:15; 7:26; 1 Pet. 2:22; 1 John 3:5).
      8. Jesus Christ had names and titles associated with His humanity: “the man Christ Jesus” (1 Tim. 2:5); “Son of Man” (Matt. 20:28); “A man of sorrows” (Isa. 53:3); “son of David” (Matt. 1:1); and “Jesus” (Matt. 1:16).
  6. The purpose of the hypostatic union: Jesus Christ had to become perfect humanity!important;
    1. To be our savior (Heb. 2:14–15; Phil. 2:7–8).
    2. To be our great high priest to represent man to God (Heb. 7:14—8:2; 10:5, 10–14).
    3. To be the mediator between God and man (Job 9:2, 32–33; 1 Tim. 2:5–6).
    4. To fulfill the promise of the Davidic covenant that David’s son would rule forever (2 Sam. 7:8–16; Ps. 89:20–37).
  7. Impeccability of the hypostatic union:
    1. As the God-man, Jesus Christ is both non posse peccare and posse non peccare. These Latin phrases refer to the impeccability of Jesus Christ.
      1. Non posse peccare means “not able to sin” and refers to the deity of Jesus Christ.
        1. As God, Jesus Christ is not able to sin, solicit to sin, compromise with sin, or have anything to do with sin except to judge it.
        2. The deity of Christ is neither temptable nor peccable.
      2. Posse non peccare means “able not to sin” and refers to the humanity of Jesus Christ.
        1. By means of the sustaining ministry of God the Holy Spirit and maximum Bible doctrine resident in His soul, Jesus Christ resisted every temptation to sin and remained absolutely perfect.
        2. The humanity of Christ was temptable, but impeccable.
    2. During the Incarnation, Jesus Christ in hypostatic union was temptable but impeccable (Heb. 4:15).
  8. The communion of attributes:
    1. Whatever the Bible says about either the divine or human nature of Christ must be attributed to the entire person, yet without transfer of attributes.
      1. Some attributes are true of His whole person: prophet, priest, and king (Matt. 11:28; John 14:6; Heb. 10:5–10; 1 Cor. 11:24; 2 Cor. 5:21).
      2. Some attributes are true only of His deity but the whole person is the subject (John 8:58).
      3. Some attributes are true only of His humanity but the whole person is the subject (John 19:28).
    2. Whatever is true of either nature is true of the entire person of Christ in hypostatic union.
    3. While the characteristics of one nature are never attributed to the other nature, the attributes of both natures contribute to the Person. This is why the Lord in hypostatic union could be both weak and omnipotent, increasing in knowledge and omniscient, finite and infinite during the First Advent.
    4. The predicates of each nature:
      1. The person of Christ is described according to His divine nature, but that which is predicated or affirmed is an attribute of His human nature. In Revelation 1:18a, the deity and glory of Christ is described, yet Christ is revealed as “I was dead, and behold, I am alive forevermore.”
      2. The person of Christ is described according to His human nature, but the predicate states the divine nature. In John 6:62, “the Son of Man” is a title of the humanity of Christ while “ascending where He was before” refers to His divine nature.
      3. The person of Christ is described according to His divine nature, but the predicate states both natures. In John 5:25–27, Christ, as “the Son of God,” a reference to His deity, resurrects the dead and as “the Son of Man,” a reference to his humanity, executes judgment from hypostatic union, from both natures.
      4. The person of Christ is described according to His human nature, but the predicate states both natures. On the cross when Jesus Christ screamed the words, “MY GOD, MY GOD, WHY HAST THOU FORSAKEN ME?” (Matt. 27:46), the humanity of Christ was being judged for the sins of the world, yet both natures were present. There was no separation of the humanity and deity of Christ on the cross. Christ, as God, was present because deity is omnipresent, both immanent and transcendent.
  9. Biblical documentation for the hypostatic union:
    1. John 1:1–2: “In a beginning which was not a beginning there always existed the Word [deity of Christ], and the Word was face-to-face with God, and the Word was God. He was in the beginning with God. All things came into being by Him, and apart from Him nothing came into being that has come into being” (corrected translation).

The conclusion of the prehistoric angelic conflict in heaven yielded two opposing forces: those angelic creatures who were elect (who chose for God) and those who were fallen (who chose against God). The true size of these angelic armies is indeterminable but likely in the hundreds of millions. Of the elect angels God said thousands upon thousands (Ps. 68:17a); and in Gethsemane Jesus said, "Or do you think that I cannot appeal to My Father, and He will at once put at My disposal more than twelve legions of angels?" (Matt. 26:53). The Apostle John "heard the voice of many angels around the throne … and the number of them was myriads of myriads, and thousands of thousands" (Rev. 5:11).
    2. These myriads of elect angels in the heavenly protocol are a well-defined organization. Created to glorify God, they perform various duties in heaven and on Earth, heralding and executing His plans. Seraphim are specifically associated with the throne of God.

They are commanded by Jesus Christ, "the Lord of hosts" (Ps. 46:7), and composed of three descending ranks: the six-winged seraphim, who lead in worship and humility in God's holiness; the four-winged cherubim, who guard God's holiness; and the wingless elect angels, who serve as messengers, guardians, and soldiers.

    1. As leaders of heavenly worship, to glorify God.

Doctrine of Imputation

  1. Definition: Imputation means to reckon, to attribute, to ascribe, or to charge to one’s account.
  2. While Jesus Christ was on the cross, God the Father imputed all the sins of the human race to Him (2 Cor. 5:21).
  3. Because the sins of the human race were imputed to Christ on the cross, God is free to impute His perfect righteousness to the believer at the moment of faith in Christ (Rom. 3:22).
  4. Abraham is the pattern of imputation of divine righteousness for all believers (Gen. 15:6; Rom. 4:3).
    1. He believed or had faith in the Lord.
    2. God counted, reckoned, or imputed it to him as righteousness.
    3. Divine righteousness is imputed only on the basis of faith alone in Christ alone (Rom. 3:22).
  5. Many Gentiles during Old Testament times attained God’s righteousness by believing in Christ, while many Jews seeking righteousness from obedience to the Mosaic Law never attained the righteousness of God (Rom. 9:30–33).
  6. Imputation is the basis of justification (Rom. 4:22; 5:1).
  7. The imputation of perfect righteousness establishes the potential for great blessing in both time and eternity.

Doctrine of Grace

  1. Definition
    1. Grace is all that God is free to do for man on the basis of the work of Jesus Christ on the cross.
    2. Grace depends on the essence or character of God.
    3. Grace is what God can do for man and still be consistent with His own character. This is illustrated by the doctrine of propitiation. Propitiation means “to satisfy” and is God’s view of the cross. God the Father is satisfied with the work of Christ on the cross on behalf of sinful man. Thus, God blesses the believer with salvation, spiritual assets for the Christian life, and eternity in heaven based on Christ’s perfect work, not our work.
  2. The divine policy of grace is the precedent for the Christian way of living and giving. God supplies the apparatus for assimilating doctrine (GAP) as the basis upon which believers live. God also provides the material resources from which believers give. The result is living and giving that reflects His plan. Grace orientation is the bona fide motivation and attitude for giving (2 Cor. 8:9).
  3. Grace is the antithesis of legalism. Legalism is man’s attempt to observe a strict standard of behavior and thereby gain the approval of God. Even if the standards are true, man’s work cannot bring him to God. Legalism is the enemy of grace.
  4. God has provided everything for salvation. Man responds in a totally nonmeritorious manner: by faith (Rom. 3:22–24; 5:19–21; Eph. 2:8–9).
  5. Under the concept of grace, the greatest thing that God can do for the saved person is to make him exactly like His Son, Jesus Christ (Rom. 8:29). This is sanctification, which is accomplished in three stages:
    1. Positional sanctification: Every believer at the point of salvation is entered into union with Christ through the baptism of the Spirit (1 Cor. 12:13). In His resurrected humanity Christ is seated at the right hand of the Father (Eph. 1:20). As one with Christ every believer shares His exalted position (Eph. 2:6).
    2. Experiential sanctification: This is the Christian way of life, the spiritual growth of believers through the consistent intake of Bible doctrine. The result: continual progress in the process of maturing spiritually (Eph. 4:11–16).
    3. Ultimate sanctification: The final stage of sanctification promotes all Church Age believers to a state of perfection and glorification in eternity. All receive resurrection bodies like that of Christ (Phil. 3:21; 2 Thess. 2:14; 1 John 3:2).3
  6. Every believer has “tasted the kindness [grace] of the Lord” at least once (1 Pet. 2:3). This verse refers to the point of salvation, when the believer receives at least forty grace gifts from God, which are unearned and undeserved.4
  7. Disorientation to grace is the believer’s greatest occupational hazard (Gal. 5:4). Falling from grace is not loss of salvation, but it is rejecting grace and accepting legalism or some other human system of pseudosanctification.
  8. God is constantly waiting to pour out His grace on every believer (Isa. 30:18–19).
  9. The Christian way of life expresses grace in many ways, for example: prayer (Heb. 4:16); suffering (2 Cor. 12:9–10); growth (2 Pet. 3:18); stability (1 Pet. 5:12); the modus vivendi of the Christian life (2 Cor. 1:12; Heb. 12:28); and the production of divine good (1 Cor. 15:10; 2 Cor. 6:1).
  10. Grace is sufficient for enduring all suffering. Through grace God is able to bless the believer in the midst of pressure, hardship, and adversity (2 Cor. 12:7–10).
  11. The axioms of grace
    1. God is perfect; His plan is perfect.
    2. A perfect plan can originate only from a perfect God.
    3. If imperfect man can do anything meritorious, then God’s plan is no longer perfect. Grace, which excludes human merit, protects the perfection of God’s plan.
    4. A plan is like a chain, which is no stronger than its weakest link. Grace excludes human merit, human ability, human good, and, therefore, the plan of God has no weak links.
    5. Human works cannot accomplish the plan of God. Human virtue always falls short of God’s absolute standards.
    6. Human good, when assumed to be man’s approach to God, expresses arrogance.
  12. Arrogance rejects grace
    1. The arrogance of the believer who rejects the doctrine of eternal security: he assumes his sins are greater than the plan of God.
    2. The arrogance of the believer who succumbs to pressure and adversity: he assumes his sufferings are greater than grace provision.
    3. The arrogance of the pseudospiritual believer: he assumes his human system is greater than the power of the Holy Spirit and the Christian way of life.
    4. The arrogance of the believer whose emotions dominate his thinking: he assumes his feelings and experiences are more valid than Bible doctrine.

Doctrine of Justification

  1. Definition: Justification is the judicial act of God whereby He declares the believer righteous at the moment of faith in Christ. It is a legal declaration, not a process of making righteous (Rom. 3:24; 5:1; Titus 3:7).
  2. Justification is based on the imputation of God's perfect righteousness to the believer at salvation (Rom. 3:22; 4:3–5; Phil. 3:9b).
    1. God credits His own perfect righteousness to the believer (2 Cor. 5:21).
    2. This imputation occurs simultaneously with faith in Christ (Rom. 4:5).
  3. Justification is by grace through faith alone, apart from works (Rom. 3:28; 4:4–5; Gal. 2:16; Eph. 2:8–9).
    1. No human merit or works contribute to justification (Rom. 4:2; Titus 3:5).
    2. It is a gift of God (Rom. 3:24).
  4. Justification removes the barrier of sin and provides eternal security (Rom. 5:1; 8:1, 33–34).
    1. There is now no condemnation for those in Christ (Rom. 8:1).
    2. God is the justifier; no one can reverse His declaration (Rom. 8:33).
  5. Justification is part of the saving work of Christ on the cross (Rom. 5:9).
    1. Christ's righteousness is imputed because His work satisfied God's justice (Rom. 3:25–26).
  6. Old Testament believers were justified by faith in the revealed Word of God, looking forward to the Messiah (Gen. 15:6; Hab. 2:4; Rom. 4:3; Gal. 3:6–9).
  7. Justification results in peace with God and access to grace (Rom. 5:1–2).

Doctrine of Unlimited Atonement

  1. Definition: Unlimited atonement is the doctrine that Jesus Christ died spiritually on the cross for the sins of the entire human race—every sin of every person in history was judged and borne by Christ (Isa. 53:5–6; 2 Cor. 5:14–15, 19; 1 Tim. 2:6; 4:10; Titus 2:11; Heb. 2:9; 1 John 2:2; 4:14).
  2. Unlimited atonement is the basis for the universal offer of salvation. God desires all men to be saved and provided the means through Christ's work (John 3:16; 1 Tim. 2:4; 2 Pet. 3:9).
  3. Christ's death was efficacious for believers (actual salvation) but unlimited in scope (provision for all).
    1. The barrier of sin is removed for all mankind, making salvation available to anyone who believes (John 1:29; Rom. 5:18; 1 John 2:2).
    2. Belief in Christ is the only condition for receiving the benefits of the atonement (John 3:18, 36; Acts 16:31).
  4. Unlimited atonement resolves the angelic conflict by demonstrating God's perfect justice and love: justice in judging sin, love in providing salvation for all (Rom. 3:25–26).
  5. Unlimited atonement does not mean universal salvation—rejection of Christ results in condemnation (John 3:18; Heb. 10:26–29).
  6. Key verses emphasizing unlimited scope:
    1. “Behold, the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world!” (John 1:29).
    2. “For the love of Christ controls us, having concluded this, that one died for all” (2 Cor. 5:14).
    3. “Who gave Himself as a ransom for all” (1 Tim. 2:6).
    4. “He Himself is the propitiation for our sins; and not for ours only, but also for those of the whole world” (1 John 2:2).
  7. Unlimited atonement is distinguished from limited atonement theories, which restrict Christ's death to the elect only. The Bible teaches provision for all, efficacy for believers.

Doctrine of Position in Christ

  1. Definition: Position in Christ is the believer's eternal, unbreakable union with the Lord Jesus Christ through the baptism of the Holy Spirit at salvation. Every Church Age believer is placed into Christ, sharing His position, privileges, and destiny (Rom. 8:1; 1 Cor. 12:13; Eph. 1:3–6; Col. 2:10).
  2. Mechanics of position in Christ:
    1. The baptism of the Holy Spirit occurs at the moment of faith in Christ, entering the believer into permanent union with Christ (Acts 1:5; 1 Cor. 12:13; Gal. 3:27–28).
    2. This union creates a new spiritual species, the “new creature” in Christ (2 Cor. 5:17; Gal. 6:15).
    3. Position in Christ is retroactive (identification with Christ's death/resurrection) and current (seated with Him at the right hand of God) (Rom. 6:3–5; Eph. 2:6; Col. 3:1–3).
  3. Characteristics of position in Christ:
    1. Eternal security—no condemnation (Rom. 8:1, 38–39).
    2. Access to God and every spiritual blessing (Eph. 1:3; Heb. 4:16).
    3. Sharing Christ's righteousness, sonship, heirship, election, predestination, and royalty (Rom. 8:16–17; Eph. 1:4–5; 2 Tim. 2:12).
    4. Deliverance from the power of the sin nature positionally (Rom. 6:6–7; Col. 2:11).
    5. Partnership with Christ in life, position, service, suffering, and destiny (Col. 3:4; 2 Tim. 2:11–12).
  4. Positional truth vs. experiential truth:
    1. Positional truth is absolute and unchanging—true regardless of the believer's spiritual status (carnal or spiritual).
    2. Experiential truth depends on the believer's daily function under the filling of the Holy Spirit and intake of doctrine.
    3. Positional truth is the foundation for experiential sanctification (growth to maturity) (Eph. 4:1; Phil. 3:12–14).
  5. Results of position in Christ:
    1. Equal privilege and opportunity for all Church Age believers (no spiritual aristocracy) (Gal. 3:28; Col. 3:11).
    2. Basis for the royal priesthood and ambassadorship (1 Pet. 2:9; 2 Cor. 5:20).
    3. Guaranteed resurrection and eternal inheritance (1 Cor. 15:51–57; Eph. 1:11, 14).
  6. Unique to the Church Age: No believer before or after the Church Age is placed in union with Christ in this way (Eph. 3:1–6; Col. 1:25–27).

Doctrine of Propitiation

  1. Definition: Propitiation is God's complete satisfaction with the person and work of Jesus Christ on the cross. God the Father is fully satisfied (propitiated) with Christ's substitutionary spiritual death as payment for the sins of the entire human race (Rom. 3:25; Heb. 2:17; 1 John 2:2; 4:10).
  2. Propitiation is directed toward God the Father, not man. It addresses God's perfect righteousness and justice, which demand judgment for sin (Rom. 3:25–26).
  3. Key aspects of propitiation:
    1. Christ's work on the cross satisfied the Father's holiness and justice (1 John 2:2).
    2. Propitiation removes the barrier of sin, making salvation available to all mankind (unlimited atonement) (Heb. 2:9; 1 John 4:10).
    3. God is free to offer salvation by grace through faith alone without compromising His essence (Rom. 3:24–26).
  4. Propitiation vs. expiation:
    1. Expiation is the cancellation of sin's penalty (directed toward sin itself).
    2. Propitiation is satisfaction toward God the Father (directed toward His righteousness/justice).
  5. Biblical terms:
    1. Greek hilasmos (1 John 2:2; 4:10): propitiation/satisfaction.
    2. Greek hilasterion (Rom. 3:25; Heb. 9:5): mercy seat/propitiatory sacrifice.
  6. Results of propitiation:
    1. God is free to justify the believer by faith alone (Rom. 3:26).
    2. Reconciliation and peace with God (Rom. 5:1; Col. 1:20).
    3. Basis for the universal offer of salvation (John 3:16; 1 Tim. 2:4–6).
  7. Propitiation is one of the eight soteriological accomplishments of Christ's cross work (along with redemption, expiation, reconciliation, imputation, justification, regeneration, positional sanctification).

Doctrine of Reconciliation

  1. Definition: Reconciliation is the removal of the barrier of sin between God and mankind through the substitutionary spiritual death of Jesus Christ on the cross. God is reconciled to man; man is reconciled to God by faith in Christ (Rom. 5:10; 2 Cor. 5:18–20; Col. 1:20–21).
  2. The barrier of sin:
    1. God's perfect righteousness and justice demand judgment for sin (Rom. 3:23; 6:23).
    2. Man's sin creates enmity and separation from God (Isa. 59:2; Eph. 2:12).
    3. The barrier consists of:
      1. Penalty of sin (spiritual death).
      2. Character of God (perfect holiness/justice).
      3. Position in Adam (inherited sin nature/spiritual death).
      4. Human good/relative righteousness (unacceptable to God).
  3. Christ's work removes the barrier:
    1. Unlimited atonement: Christ bore the sins of the entire world (2 Cor. 5:19; 1 John 2:2).
    2. Redemption, expiation, propitiation satisfy God's justice (Rom. 3:24–25; Col. 2:14).
    3. God is now free to offer salvation to all without compromising His essence (2 Cor. 5:18–19).
  4. Man's response: Faith in Christ reconciles the individual to God (Rom. 5:1; Col. 1:21–22).
    1. Believers are reconciled positionally at salvation.
    2. Unbelievers remain enemies until faith (Rom. 5:10).
  5. Results of reconciliation:
    1. Peace with God (Rom. 5:1; Col. 1:20).
    2. Ambassadorship: Believers represent reconciled God to the world (2 Cor. 5:20).
    3. Basis for eternal security and spiritual life.
  6. Reconciliation is God's work, not man's:
    1. God initiates and accomplishes reconciliation (2 Cor. 5:18–19).
    2. Man receives it by non-meritorious faith.

Doctrine of the Blood of Christ

  1. Definition: The blood of Christ is a metaphorical expression representing the substitutionary spiritual death of Jesus Christ on the cross. It symbolizes the judicial judgment of the sins of the entire human race by God the Father during the three hours of darkness when Christ was spiritually separated from the Father (Matt. 27:45–46; Mark 15:33–34; Luke 23:44).
  2. The term “blood” is used anthropopathically (language of accommodation) to explain to finite man the infinite work of Christ in bearing our sins (1 Pet. 2:24; 2 Cor. 5:21).
  3. Physical death (shedding of blood) is not salvific:
    1. Christ's physical death was necessary to vacate the body for resurrection, ascension, and session, but it did not save mankind.
    2. Salvation was accomplished during the spiritual death of Christ when He was judged for sins (Isa. 53:10–11; Heb. 9:22 without blood context for spiritual death).
  4. Key principles:
    1. The blood of Christ represents the saving work of Christ on the cross—unlimited atonement (Heb. 9:12, 22; 10:10–14; 1 John 2:2).
    2. Application of the blood: Faith alone in Christ alone (Rev. 1:5; 7:14—cleansed by faith, represented as blood).
    3. The blood of Christ provides cleansing from all sin (1 John 1:7).
    4. The blood of Christ purchases the believer from the slave market of sin (redemption) (Acts 20:28; 1 Pet. 1:18–19).
    5. The blood of Christ propitiates the Father (Rom. 3:25; 1 John 2:2).
  5. Misunderstandings to avoid:
    1. Pagan literalism: The literal blood of Christ has no salvific value (as in transubstantiation or pagan rituals).
    2. Literal blood poured on mercy seat in heaven: No biblical support (Heb. 9:12 refers to His own blood as metaphor for spiritual death).
  6. Communion symbolism: The wine represents the blood (spiritual death) of Christ in remembrance (Luke 22:20; 1 Cor. 11:25–26).

Doctrine of Rebound

  1. Rebound is the grace provision for the carnal believer to recover spirituality, the filling of the Holy Spirit, through naming sins privately to God the Father. This is the only method of restoring the believer’s fellowship with God and resuming the spiritual life (Prov. 1:23; Eph. 5:14; cf. 5:18).

  3. Rebound must be understood in the light of relationship with God (Jer. 3:13), illustrated by the top circle (Rom. 8:1).
  4. The believer sins after salvation, but such sin is categorized as carnality, not loss of salvation (Rom. 8:38–39; 1 Cor. 3:1–3; 1 John 1:8, 10).
  5. The mechanics of the rebound technique: Confess the sin (1 John 1:9), isolate it (Heb. 12:15), and forget it (Phil. 3:13–14).
  6. The alternative to rebound is divine discipline (1 Cor. 11:31; Heb. 12:6).
  7. The discouragement to rebound: legalism and other Christians (Luke 15:11–32).
  8. The grace provision of rebound includes the following:
    1. The mechanics (Gal. 6:1).
    2. A mental attitude of grace (Matt. 18:23–35).
    3. Grace orientation (Col. 3:13).
    4. Reward for assisting others (James 5:19–20).
  9. Biblical synonyms for rebound:

Doctrine of Defilement by Association

  1. Association with evil or compromise with evil is a trespass or sin (Rom. 16:17–18; 1 Thess. 5:22).
  2. If you fellowship with sin, you partake of the sin (Prov. 11:27).
  3. You tend to assume the pattern of sins and weaknesses of those with whom you associate (Prov. 13:20).
  4. Two categories of association trespass.
    1. The responding trespass, in which the believer admires those who are sinning and, by association with them, is influenced toward sinfulness. This is why we are warned against associating with certain types of believers (Rom. 16:17–18; 1 Cor. 5:9–11; 15:33) as well as with unbelievers (Prov. 1:10–19; 2 Cor. 6:14–16).
    2. The reaction trespass, in which the believer reacts to a mental attitude sin. For example: Someone is implacable, so you become implacable. Someone is jealous or vindictive, so you seek to retaliate. Someone appears to be hostile to you, so you, in turn, show hostility (Prov. 24:29; Heb. 10:30).
      1. Under reaction-type trespasses, the believer lowers himself to the standard of the offender (Rom. 12:17–19).
      2. By applying Romans 12:19–20, the believer places the matter in the hands of the Lord and so avoids the association trespass (Heb. 1:9).
  5. Since interaction in the human race is inevitable, the believer should guard against association sins which would rob him of his inner peace and happiness (Rom. 12:17; 16:17).

Summary of the Doctrine of Mental Attitude

  1. What you think is what you really are. Therefore, your mental attitude determines both your life and your character (Prov. 23:7).
  2. During the angelic conflict and human history, there are two conflicting attitudes striving for control of the mentality of the soul: divine viewpoint and human viewpoint (Isa. 55:7–9).
    1. Divine viewpoint is the “mind of Christ” and the believer’s orientation to life based on the thinking, value system, and problem-solving ability gleaned from Bible doctrine.
      1. God’s plan, Operation Grace, demands a transformation of the thinking of the believer-priest to divine viewpoint (Rom. 12:2; 2 Tim. 1:7).
      2. The intake of doctrine under the daily function of GAP changes the believer’s mental attitude to divine viewpoint (1 Cor. 2:16; 2 Cor. 10:4–5; Phil. 2:5).
      3. Divine viewpoint results in the following:
        1. Confidence under all circumstances in life (2 Cor. 5:1, 6, 8).
        2. Stability in adversity or prosperity (Phil. 4:7; 2 Thess. 2:2).
        3. Inner happiness that produces capacity for life, love, and blessing (Phil. 2:2).
        4. Giving with the proper motivation of grace orientation (2 Cor. 9:7).
        5. Capacity to love God, and a relaxed mental attitude toward people (1 Cor. 13:4–8a; 1 John 4:19).
    2. Human viewpoint is any human or satanic system of thinking, values, or problem solving that is contrary and opposed to divine viewpoint, values, and problem solving as revealed in the Bible.
      1. Mental attitude sins produce self-induced misery (Prov. 15:13).
      2. Worldliness is human viewpoint mental attitude rather than overt activity (Rom. 12:2; Col. 3:2).
      3. Evil is something you think rather than something you do (Matt. 9:4; Gal. 6:3). Doing results from thinking.

Doctrine of the Heart

  1. Definition
    1. The word “heart” in the Bible (from the Hebrew, leb, and the Greek, kardia) rarely refers to the physiological heart, but it is used for that part of the mentality of the soul called the “right lobe,” the locale for thinking divine viewpoint (1 Sam. 16:7; Prov. 23:7; James 3:14).
    2. The usage of the word “heart” for the right lobe of the soul is based on the analogy to the physical heart. Just as the heart is the pump that circulates blood throughout the body and thereby supports physical life, so the right lobe of the soul circulates Bible doctrine that supports the spiritual life of the believer.
    3. The heart or right lobe is the target of GAP, the source of concentration and application of doctrine to experience (1 Kings 3:9, 12; Ps. 19:14; 119:11; Prov. 2:2, 10; 3:3; 4:4; 10:8; 15:14, 18:15).
  2. The essence of the heart
    1. The frame of reference is the first compartment of the right lobe where doctrine enters and becomes epignosis knowledge. Here, doctrine accumulates gradually in increments and is used by the Holy Spirit to increase the believer’s capacity to learn more doctrine. Truth builds upon truth, one doctrine upon another. The frame of reference not only retains doctrine but cycles it to the other compartments of the right lobe.
    2. The memory center is where epignosis doctrine is stored and recalled (Ps. 119:16, 93). Recall has nothing to do with a good human memory but with the inculcation or repetition of doctrine and the mentorship of the Holy Spirit (John 14:20). The memory center supplies pertinent doctrines for all other compartments (Lam. 3:20–21; 2 Cor. 10:4–6; 2 Pet. 3:2).
    3. The vocabulary storage is the dictionary of the soul. This compartment accumulates technical theological words and concepts to define God, man, salvation, the Christian way of life, and every other category of doctrine. Thinking demands vocabulary. Once these theological terms are mastered, they work together with the frame of reference and memory center to develop the believer’s ability to think divine viewpoint (Deut. 8:3; Jer. 15:16; Matt. 4:4).
    4. Categorical storage organizes principles of doctrine from the frame of reference and vocabulary storage. Scripture is compared with Scripture so that doctrine is classified according to subject matter. Knowledge is built on knowledge. Categorical storage becomes the basis for understanding more advanced doctrines (Prov. 2:2, 10; 3:3; 15:14–15; 18:15).
    5. The conscience is where norms and standards reside. It is the faculty of the soul that separates right from wrong, establishes priorities, and guides and regulates life. A strong conscience has standards built upon divine viewpoint, while a weak conscience is acquired from human viewpoint (Dan. 1:8; Acts 24:16; Rom. 2:14–15; 13:5; 1 Cor. 8:7; 2 Cor. 4:2; 5:11; 1 Tim. 4:1–2; 2 Tim. 1:3; Titus 1:15; Heb. 9:14; 1 Pet. 2:18–19; 3:14–16).5
    6. Momentum combines epignosis doctrine with the power of the filling of the Holy Spirit to motivate and accelerate spiritual growth (2 Pet. 3:18).
    7. Wisdom is the result of epignosis knowledge and motivation from all other compartments of the right lobe. In this final compartment, the thinking of epignosis doctrine is converted into action—divine viewpoint applied to the circumstances of life. Wisdom builds the edification complex for living the spiritual life (Prov. 3:21–22; 2 Cor. 10:4–6; Col. 1:9; James 3:17).

Doctrine of Days

The word “day” is used in the Bible in three ways. The context of the Scripture always makes the meaning explicit.

  1. A day shorter than twenty-four hours:
    1. The “DAY OF SALVATION” describes that moment when an individual believes in the Lord Jesus Christ (2 Cor. 6:2).
    2. The “day of Christ” refers to the Rapture of the Church which will be instantaneous, “in the twinkling of an eye” (1 Cor. 15:52; Phil. 1:10; 2:16).
    3. The “day of wrath” occurs when the unbeliever is judged by Jesus Christ at the Great White Throne Judgment and is cast into the lake of fire (Rom. 2:5; Rev. 20:11–15).
    4. The “day of redemption” refers to the believer’s ultimate sanctification or redemption of the body at resurrection, the Rapture of the Church (Rom. 8:23; Eph. 4:30).
  2. A day longer than twenty-four hours:
    1. The “day of the LORD” can have one of two meanings, depending on the context:
      1. The combined period including the Tribulation, Second Advent, and Millennium, approximately 1,007 years
      2. Any one segment of the Tribulation, Second Advent, or Millennium (Isa. 13:6; Joel 1:15; 2:1; Zech. 14:1; 1 Thess. 5:2; 2 Pet. 3:10)
    2. The “day of God” refers to eternity (2 Pet. 3:12).
  3. A twenty-four-hour day (a solar day):
    1. The six days of the earth’s restoration which were literal twenty-four-hour days (Gen. 1:3–31)
    2. The Sabbath, a special day of rest and worship in the Dispensation of Israel (Ex. 20:10)
    3. “The Lord’s day,” Sunday in the Dispensation of the Church (Rev. 1:10)
    4. Living one day at a time in the Christian way of life (Rom. 14:5–6)
    5. The “all the day” concept of learning Bible doctrine daily (Ps. 119:97)

Doctrine of the Interpretations of History

  1. Geographical Interpretation
    1. The theory that topography determines or explains the course of a nation or humanity.
    2. Rivers, valleys, mountains, weather, etc., have influence on a people; e.g., the development of the Greek city-states influenced by the surrounding mountains.
  2. Biological Interpretation
    1. The attempt to explain the direction of mankind in terms of competition for food, mates, or power. Man is subject to his heredity and culture.
    2. This theory was expounded by Thomas Malthus in his “Essay on Population” (1798): If human life is too numerous for food supply, nature has three agents to restore the balance—famine, pestilence, and war.
  3. Racial Interpretation
    1. The Aryan-Nordic theory: superiority of race (first advanced as a serious concept in writing by the Count Joseph Arthur de Gobineau and taken to its conclusion by Adolph Hitler).
    2. The twofold fallacy of this theory: purity of race no longer exists; the theory becomes a satanic device to set up one person or people over another.
  4. Genius Interpretation
    1. The rise of a super-genius to stabilize a desperate situation in an epoch of history.
    2. For example: great men such as Alexander the Great, Julius Caesar, or the Apostle Paul influencing the course of world history.
    3. God raises up men of genius to stabilize a nation—Moses, Jeremiah, Daniel, even Gentile kings such as Cyrus the Great.
  5. Morality Interpretation
    1. The rise or fall of nations based on their degree of virtue and morality.
    2. For example: the rise of the Jewish nation due to its laws of morality (Ten Commandments); the fall of the Canaanites resulting from degeneracy; the decline of Greece on the basis of immorality; the rise of Rome through establishment principles. (Although based on observation, this theory must be related to principles of doctrine in order to be valid.)
  6. Economic Interpretation
    1. The definition of history in terms of economics in action (propounded by Karl Marx).
    2. For example: the Trojan War explained by the Greek’s commercial control of the Dardanelles; the Crusades explained by Western attempts to capture trade routes to the East; the French Revolution explained by the rise of a middle class, which needed economic and legislative freedom for trade and enterprise. (Fallacy arises when a correct observation excludes Jesus Christ.)
  7. Divine Interpretation
    1. Divine sovereignty and omnipotence in the affairs of mankind must be recognized.
    2. While the foregoing human interpretations of history contain elements of truth based on observation, they all fall short because they are improperly applied. They ignore or reject Jesus Christ who controls history and who is the center of history; in fact, who is history.
    3. Only dispensations, or the divine interpretation of history, present the complete picture.
    4. Divine sovereignty and the free will of man coexist in God's plan for history. God's sovereign divine plan in conjunction with the free will of man determine the course of history. Neither human failure nor the satanic confusion of cosmos diabolicus will deter the completion of history in the manner that God has decreed.

    4. The redemption money for the firstborn in Israel (Num. 18:16)

Summary of the Doctrine of Giving

Doctrine of Happiness

  1. General Definition
    1. Happiness is defined by Webster's Third New International Dictionary as "a state of well-being characterized by relative permanence, by dominantly agreeable emotion ranging in value from mere contentment to intense joy in living and by a natural desire for its continuation; a pleasurable or enjoyable experience. . . . Happiness is the general term denoting enjoyment of or pleasurable satisfaction in well-being, security or fulfillment of wishes."
    2. The common perception of happiness centers around emotional stimulation derived from pleasant experience and "fulfillment of wishes." Human beings typically regard favorable environment, personal success and recognition, harmonious relationships, and the general absence of adversity as necessary for attaining the state of happiness. Marked by "relative permanence," this happiness endures only as long as the pleasant circumstances remain.
  2. Biblical Perspective of Happiness
    1. Genuine happiness is not emotional stimulation but a way of thinking formed from truth in the soul. As such truth replaces arrogance with humility, there results stability of mind, an attitude of contentment, a sense of honor and appreciation for life, and freedom from fear and anxiety, even under pressure. These characteristics translate to the mental attitude of happiness as described in Scripture.
    2. God provides truth for genuine happiness in two categories: the laws of divine establishment, for all mankind, and Bible doctrine, for spiritual growth of the believer in Jesus Christ.13
      1. Both believer and unbeliever grounded in divine establishment truth can experience genuine happiness on a limited basis.
      2. The believer oriented to the spiritual truths in God's Word can possess maximum genuine happiness. This greatest happiness is attained by consistent residence in God's grace plan, utilizing the power of God the Holy Spirit and applying metabolized doctrine to life.
  3. The Happiness of God
    1. God exists permanently in a state of perfect happiness (Rom. 1:25b). God's own happiness reflects His essence and is therefore based solely on who and what God is. Since God is eternal and immutable and possesses absolute righteousness, His happiness is never-ending, unchanging, and perfect in nature.
    2. God's happiness cannot be tarnished or in any way challenged or destroyed. He can never be saddened, disappointed, or dissatisfied. Neither the failure of angels or man nor their rejection of God and His provision can cause unhappiness in Him.
    3. God is the source of true happiness (Ps. 43:4a). In eternity past, God designed a grace plan whereby He can share His perfect happiness with man in time. This is possible only through man's regeneration and subsequent perception of Bible doctrine (Hab. 3:18; Matt. 4:4; John 17:17; cf. 13:17; James 1:25).
  4. Three Categories of Happiness for Man
    1. Neutral Happiness—Neutral H
      1. Neutral happiness (neutral H) belongs to the person who possesses humility derived from the laws of divine establishment. Compliance with the laws of establishment allows the person to move beyond basic morality to a place of human honor and integrity. Self-discipline, personal responsibility, courage, respect for authority, patriotism, and other establishment-oriented virtues characterize the person with neutral happiness.
      2. Neutral H, though genuine, is a limited, temporary happiness dependent upon the virtue of the individual and the perpetuation of establishment principles within his environment.
      3. Neutral H is available to both believer and unbeliever for honorable function within certain establishment-related areas of life.
        1. The unbeliever can enjoy being married to someone with whom he has mental and physical compatibility and can also find satisfaction in the honorable execution of a job or profession (Eccl. 9:9).
        2. Parents who teach their children establishment principles will be gratified and honored by the virtue of their offspring (Prov. 23:24–25).
        3. Both believer and unbeliever who live under the laws of divine establishment can find neutral happiness in national freedom and prosperity and in the role of military victory (Ps. 89:15–18; 144:15).
      4. Where regard for establishment principles does not exist, neutral H cannot exist, because there is no authority orientation present to restrain arrogance (Prov. 29:18).
      5. Neutral happiness has no perpetuation beyond the grave. Regardless of the honor and happiness attained in the temporal life, the unbeliever, lacking the imputed righteousness of God from faith in Jesus Christ, is consigned upon death to eternity in the lake of fire (Rev. 20:15).
    2. Pseudohappiness—Minus H
      1. Pseudohappiness (minus H) is the superficial, fleeting pleasure and emotional stimulation derived from environment, human accomplishments, circumstances, wealth, possessions, or people.
      2. Lacking a foundation in spiritual or establishment truth, pseudohappiness is volatile, transient, and frustrating, incapable of providing real solutions or sustaining anyone through the challenges and adversities of life.
      3. Satan, through his cosmic system, encourages pseudohappiness as a substitute for the genuine happiness that only God can provide.14 Satan lures people from the plan of God with systems of stimulation and self-indulgence that either counterfeit or cancel out genuine happiness.
        1. The cosmic system appeals to man's sin nature with the myth that the details of life—money, success, pleasure, social life, relationships, sex, material things, human approbation, status symbols, health—can alleviate unhappiness and provide lasting fulfillment.
        2. Satan's most effective pseudohappiness lure is religion—empty rituals, human works, emotion-based worship, and strict behavior through which Christians ignorant of doctrine seek happiness.
      4. Pseudohappiness describes the believer who rejects the spiritual life and embarks on a frantic search for happiness in the details of life.15
        1. Believers on a frantic search are described in 2 Timothy 3:4 as "lovers of pleasure rather than lovers of God." With desire for stimulation exceeding their desire for Bible doctrine, they build their lives around pleasure goals and neglect goals related to spiritual advance.
        2. The believer frantically searching for happiness caters to the lust patterns of the sin nature; he indulges in a lifestyle of self-gratification through legalism, licentiousness, or both (Gal. 5:16).16
      5. Pseudohappiness is a source of self-induced misery as well as a guarantee for divine discipline (Ps. 7:14–16).
        1. The search for happiness apart from God and His plan is a vicious cycle that always arrives back at the same place—boredom, restlessness, instability, confusion, and emptiness (Prov. 14:14; Eccl. 1:5–9; 2 Tim. 3:7). Bad decisions made in every frenzied pursuit of happiness only intensify the misery that motivated the search in the first place.
        2. The believer immersed in pseudohappiness warrants God the Father's corrective discipline, administered to encourage rebound and spiritual advance (Prov. 3:12; Heb. 12:6). Persistent failure to respond to divine punitive measures results in the sin unto death: the believer who lives with minus H will die with minus H; no dying grace, just misery in dying (Prov. 14:12–13; 1 John 5:16b).

This happiness is an enduring state of inner peace, confidence, and contentment unrelated to any detail or circumstance of life. The believer with plus H has the happiness of God residing in his soul.

Doctrine of Evil

  1. Definition.

    2. Just as grace and doctrine represent the genius and thinking of God, so evil represents the subtle genius and thinking of Satan; therefore, evil is a system of thought just as doctrine is a system of thought.
    3. Evil is also the function of the sin nature as the ruler of human life (Rom. 6:2).
    4. Evil is the distortion of Bible doctrine and the laws of divine establishment (Ps. 50:16).
    5. When either sin or human good are intensified, they become evil.

    1. Only sin was judged on the cross (1 Pet. 2:24).

  3. There is no evil in God (Ps. 5:4; James 1:13).

Seven Deaths

  1. Physical death is the separation of the soul from the body:
    1. When the believer dies, God separates the real person—the immortal, immaterial soul and human spirit—from the body and enters him into the presence of the Lord (2 Cor. 5:8).19
    2. Physical death occurs when the electrical impulses of the brain can no longer be detected. Physical death cannot be gauged by the electrical impulses recorded from the heart.
    3. Physical death is not a tragedy for the believer but a distinct glory (Phil. 1:21).
    4. The unbeliever has no future with God. When he dies, his soul leaves his body and goes to a place called "Torments" in Hades. This is the temporary residence for the unbeliever where he awaits the Last Judgment and condemnation to permanent residence in the lake of fire (Rev. 20:14).
  2. Spiritual death is separation from God in time—divine condemnation at the point of physical birth (Gen. 2:17; Rom. 5:12; 6:23; Eph. 2:1).
    1. Spiritual death was the result of Adam's original sin when he lost his human spirit and fellowship with God. He was no longer able to comprehend spiritual phenomena.
    2. Spiritual death for all subsequent generations is the result of the imputation at birth of Adam's original sin to the genetically formed sin nature. Every person is born physically alive but spiritually dead (Rom. 5:12, 18; 6:23).
    3. Faith in the Lord Jesus Christ in time takes care of the problem of spiritual death (1 Cor. 15:22; 2 Cor. 5:17).
  3. The second death refers to the Last Judgment, or separation from God for all eternity (Heb. 9:27; Rev. 20:12–15).
  4. Positional death refers to identification with Christ's death. We are identified with Christ, not only in His death on the cross (retroactive positional truth), but also in His ascension and session at the right hand of the Father (current positional truth).20 (Rom. 6:1–14; Col. 2:12; 3:3).
  5. Temporal death is the believer's separation from fellowship with God in time.
    1. When the believer gets out of fellowship with God through sin—mental or overt—he is in status quo carnality, frequently referred to as "death" (Luke 15:24, 32; Rom. 8:6; Eph. 5:14; 1 Tim. 5:6; James 1:15; Rev. 3:1).
    2. First John 1:9 is the means by which the believer who is out of fellowship can regain fellowship with God.
  6. Operational death is any form of Christian service apart from the filling of the Holy Spirit—human good produced by the carnal believer (Heb. 6:1; James 2:26).
  7. Sexual death is the inability to procreate. It is mentioned twice in Scripture: once in Romans 4:17–21 and again in Hebrews 11:11–12. Both occurrences are related to Abraham.

  2. Reversionism is the cause of the sin unto death (Jer. 9:13–16; 44:12; Phil. 3:18–19; Rev. 3:15–16).

Doctrine of Money

  1. Money was designed as a medium of exchange. Coined money was unknown until seventh century B.C. Before coins, valuable metal was measured in terms of ingots or rings. Croesus, king of Lydia, was the first to preserve his money in the form of coins. When Cyrus the Great conquered Lydia, he adopted the concept of coinage. Cyrus spread the concept throughout the Persian Empire and beyond.
  2. Monetary transactions are a legitimate function of life (Gen. 23:9; Jer. 32:44).
  3. The giving of money is an expression of the believer's priesthood (1 Cor. 16:2; 2 Cor. 8–9; Heb. 13:15–16).
  4. Materialism lust makes money a god and enslaves a person to the details of life (Matt. 6:24; Luke 16:9, 11, 13). Money is a useful and helpful servant, but a harsh and cruel master.
  5. The dangers of money to the unbeliever
    1. Salvation cannot be purchased with money (Mark 8:36–37). Salvation has been paid in full by the blood (spiritual death) of Christ (1 Pet. 1:18–19).
    2. Money can cause the rich man to put his trust in the wrong things (Mark 10:23–25).
    3. Money can hinder the unbeliever from seeking salvation (Luke 16:19–31).
    4. Money has no credit with God (Prov. 11:4, 28; 13:7, 11).
  6. The danger of money to the believer
    1. Money can become a part of slavery to the details of life (Eccl. 5:10–6:2).
    2. Love of money becomes the root of all kinds of evil (1 Tim. 6:6–12, 17–19).
    3. Money can destroy grace orientation and contribute to reversionism (Acts 5:1–10; James 4:14–5:6).
  7. Money can be a source of deceit (Jude 11; cf. Num. 22—24).
  8. Illusions about money:
    1. Money is a means of happiness. For the believer there is no happiness from the details of life, including money. The believer's happiness is built on the intake of doctrine, spiritual growth, and spiritual maturity.
    2. Money is a means of security. Grace provides a security for which there is no substitute (Matt. 6:24–33).
    3. Money can buy anything. There are many things which money cannot buy: salvation, love, happiness, stability, or tranquility.

Doctrine of the Fifth Part

  1. Man gains through grace: This applies to the believer who is wronged and puts the matter in the Lord's hands. Such a believer becomes the gainer. "Never avenge yourselves, but leave it to the wrath of God, for it is written, 'Vengeance is mine, I will repay, says the Lord'" (Rom. 12:19).
  2. Man gains more by salvation than he lost in the Fall: In his Fall, man was lower than angels, but through salvation, with God's justice, he became positionally higher than the angels (Heb. 1:14).
  3. Application: Man also gains by the cross. "How will He [God the Father] not also with Him [Jesus Christ] freely [graciously] give us [believers] all things?" (Rom. 8:32b).
  4. Illustration: When wronged through sins of the tongue, the believer becomes the gainer when he leaves judgment to God (Matt. 7:2).
  5. Gain through rebound: The believer who rebounds gains blessing. Even if his suffering continues, the cursing of discipline is turned to blessing (Rom. 8:35–37).
  6. Cursing turned to blessing: The added one-fifth in the rebound offering denotes the replacement of cursing with blessing. On the cross, Jesus Christ removed our curse and substituted blessing (Rom. 8:31).
  7. Conclusion
    1. The believer who understands grace is not disturbed by the instabilities of mankind.
    2. The believer who lives by grace always gains, even though he appears to be losing (Rom. 8:35).
    3. Grace cannot lose in sin, adversity, suffering, or prosperity.
      1. Grace in phase one: salvation (Ps. 103:8–12; Rom. 3:23–24; 4:4; 5:20; 2 Cor. 8:9; Eph. 2:8–9; Titus 2:11; 3:7; Heb. 2:9)
      2. Grace in phase two: the spiritual life, provision, and sustenance (1 Cor. 15:10; 2 Cor. 6:1; 12:9; 2 Tim. 2:1; 1 Pet. 5:12; 2 Pet. 3:18); greater grace to the spiritually mature (Heb. 12:28; James 4:6)
      3. Grace in phase three: eternity (Eph. 2:7)

Doctrine of Salt in Scripture

  1. Salt is a preserver. The Phoenicians imported fish and other goods to Judah (Neh. 13:16), and salt was used in curing and drying the fish. Salt kept foods from spoiling; therefore, salt speaks of eternal life. The gift offering together with the salt reminds us of John 3:16.

    To those in the Church Age who have believed in God's indescribable Gift, salt encompasses positional truth. One of the many assets which God gives to us at the moment of salvation is the baptism of the Spirit, whereby believers are entered into union with Christ, who is eternal life. We now share His life (1 John 5:11–12), and "neither death, nor life, nor angels, nor principalities, nor things present, nor things to come, nor powers, nor height, nor depth, nor any other created thing, shall be able to separate us from the love of God, which is in Christ Jesus our Lord" (Rom. 8:38b–39). We are thus "salted," preserved forever!

  2. Salt is a seasoner (Job 6:6). As a seasoning, salt is figurative of the believer's inner happiness response to the gift of eternal life, namely, the joy of salvation which comes from a knowledge of Bible doctrine. Salt, then, is doctrine, which seasons life (Col. 4:6). As salt makes foods palatable and tasty, so Bible doctrine makes life more flavorful because knowledge of God's truth gives the capacity for its enjoyment to the fullest.
  3. Salt by analogy is the antithesis of leaven. Salt is a preserver — true doctrine; leaven is a corrupter — false doctrine (1 Cor. 5:8; Gal. 5:9).
  4. Customs of the ancient world used salt to signify a contract or hospitality.
    1. Under the laws of hospitality: If, as an enemy of the host, you ate salt with him at the table, you were preserved from harm while in his house.
    2. As a sign of fulfilling a contract: Both parties in the contract ate salt as a symbol of agreement and abiding friendship.
  5. The eating of salt depicts the appropriation of salvation. The moment you "ate the salt" of eternal life you were saved. After salvation, the eating of salt pictures the learning of Bible doctrine.
  6. "Salted with fire" refers to eternity spent in the lake of fire (Mark 9:48–49). Since the unbeliever has no salt in him, he is disqualified from eternity with God.
  7. Believers are said to be the "salt of the earth" (Matt. 5:13).
    1. Salt acts as an unseen preservative. The presence of doctrinally oriented believers in a society or nation is the basis for its preservation (Mark 9:50).
    2. Salt provides flavor. This is the believer's impact on society by his application of Bible doctrine and his communication of the divine viewpoint of life (Col. 4:6).
  8. The "salt has become tasteless" is analogous to the believer perpetually out of fellowship and in reversionism (Matt. 5:13; Luke 14:34–35).
  9. Inferior salt was used as a fertilizer in the ancient world and as a means to speed the decomposition of dung (Matt. 5:13; cf. Luke 14:34–35).
  10. The strewing of salt over the ruins of a captured city was a sign of utter desolation and irretrievable ruin (Judg. 9:45), as with Sodom and Gomorrah (Deut. 29:23; Zeph. 2:9).
  11. Newborn babies of the ancient world were bathed and then rubbed with salt (Ezek. 16:4).
  12. Salt was used as a purifier (2 Kings 2:21).
  13. The "salt of the covenant of your God" (Lev. 2:13).
    1. This phrase refers to an ancient custom whereby treaties or agreements were ratified by a covenant of salt. Both parties ate salt out of a common dish as a symbol of mutual agreement and abiding friendship. This made the contract valid and binding, equivalent to cosignatures on a legal transaction (Num. 18:19).
    2. The "salt of the covenant of your God" applies to a contract God has provided for us: the cross. This contract is based upon the Father's propitiation and man's reconciliation. Were it not for the cross, God and man would be at enmity with each other forever (Rom. 5), but thanks to the cross, the contract contains an eternal life clause — conditional on faith alone in Christ alone (Rom. 5:8–10). The offer is still open; you can accept it or reject it.
